    Section 6.4 Other Logarithmic Functions

    Just as there is essentially only one exponential function, it should come as no surprise that there is essentially only one logarithm. We can regard the exponential function with base \(a \neq 1\) as the composition
    \begin{equation*} \Exp{a}(x) = \exp\left(\ln(a)x\right) = \exp \circ \mul{\ln(a)}(x). \end{equation*}
    The condition \(a \neq 1\) guarantees that \(\ln(a) \neq 0\) and thus we may invert \(\mul{\ln(a)}\) using the division by \(\ln(a)\) function
    \begin{equation*} \mul{\ln(a)}^{-1}(x) = \frac{x}{\ln(a)}. \end{equation*}
    By Formula 5.4.8, the inverse of the exponential with base \(a\) is the function defined by
    \begin{equation*} \mul{\ln(a)}^{-1} \circ \exp^{-1}(x) = \mul{\frac{1}{\ln(a)}} \circ \ln(x) = \frac{\ln(x)}{\ln(a)}. \end{equation*}

    Subsection 6.4.1 Changing the Base of the Logarithm

    Definition 6.4.1. The Logarithm with Base \(a\).

    Assume \(a \neq 1\) is a positive real number. The logarithm with base \(a\), is the function \(\log_a \colon (0,\infty) \to \R\text{,}\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} \log_a(x) = \frac{\ln(x)}{\ln(a)}. \end{equation*}
    In particular, for every real number \(x\) and every positive real number \(y\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \log_a \circ \Exp{a}(x) = \log_a(a^x) = x \quad\text{and}\quad \Exp{a} \circ \log_a(y) = a^{\log_a(y)} = y. \end{equation*}
    Our understanding of the natural logarithm and graph transformations allows us to very quickly visualize the logarithm with base \(a\text{.}\) Just as for exponentials, there are two cases: \(a \lt 1\) and \(1 \lt a\text{.}\) When \(a > 1\text{,}\) \(\ln(a) > 0\) and so the graph of
    \begin{equation*} y = \log_a(x) = \frac{\ln(x)}{\ln(a)} \end{equation*}
    is obtained as a vertical scaling of \(\ln(x)\) by \(1/\ln(a)\text{.}\) We have plotted the logarithm for several bases in Figure 6.4.2.
    When \(a \lt 1\text{,}\) \(\ln(a) \lt 0\text{,}\) and so the graph of
    \begin{equation*} y = \log_a(x) = \frac{\ln(x)}{\ln(a)} \end{equation*}
    is obtained by vertically scaling \(y = \ln(x)\) by \(1/\ln(a)\) and reflecting across the \(x\)-axis. We have plotted the logarithm for several bases in Figure 6.4.3.
    Explicitly defining the logarithm with base \(a\) in terms of the natural logarithm also allows us a simple way to convert between logarithms with different bases. Assume \(a \neq 1\) and \(b \neq 1\) are both positive numbers. Multiplying both sides of the equation
    \begin{equation*} \log_b(y) = \frac{\ln(y)}{\ln(b)} \end{equation*}
    by the number \(\ln(b)\) allows us to write
    \begin{equation*} \ln(y) = \ln(b)\log_b(y). \end{equation*}
    We can substitute this for \(\ln(y)\) in the definition of the logarithm base \(a\) to produce
    \begin{equation*} \log_a(y) = \frac{\ln(y)}{\ln(a)} = \frac{\ln(b)\log_b(y)}{\ln(a)}. \end{equation*}
    It is often convenient to involve only two bases in this expression, so we rewrite
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\ln(b)}{\ln(a)} = \frac{1}{\frac{\ln(a)}{\ln(b)}} = \frac{1}{\log_b(a)}. \end{equation*}

    Formula 6.4.4. Base Change for Logarithms.

    Assume \(a \neq 1\) and \(b \neq 1\) are positive real numbers. For all positive real numbers \(y\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \log_a(y) = \frac{\log_b(y)}{\log_b(a)}. \end{equation*}

    Remark 6.4.5.

    The logarithm — often written \(\log(x)\) — means different things to different people, often depending on the context.
    • Most mathematicians (and mathematical texts) will write \(\log(x)\) to mean the logarithm with base \(e\text{.}\) This is because mathematicians are primarily interested in the functions and their behaviors, not numbers or numerical data.
    • Most computer scientists will write \(\log(x)\) to mean the logarithm with base \(2\text{.}\) This is because computer scientists most frequently work with numbers and numerical data expressed in base \(2\) or binary — the number system used for computational devices. In base \(2\text{,}\) numbers are represented as strings with digits that are either \(0\) or \(1\text{.}\) For example, in base \(2\) the string \(1011\) stands for the number
      \begin{align*} 1 \cdot 2^3 + 0 \cdot 2^2 + 1 \cdot 2^1 + 1 \cdot 2^0 \amp= 1 \cdot 8 + 0 + 1 \cdot 2 + 1 \cdot 1\\ \amp= 8 + 2 + 1\\ \amp= 11 \end{align*}
    • Most scientists and engineers will write \(\log(x)\) to mean the logarithm with base \(10\text{.}\) This is because scientists and engineers most frequently work with numbers and numerical data expressed in the usual base 10 or decimal number system. In contrast to base \(2\text{,}\) the base 10 number system consists of strings of digits that are allowed to be one of \(0\text{,}\) \(1\text{,}\) \(2\text{,}\) \(3\text{,}\) \(4\text{,}\) \(5\text{,}\) \(6\text{,}\) \(7\text{,}\) \(8\text{,}\) or \(9\text{.}\) For example, in base \(10\) the string \(1234\) stands for
      \begin{equation*} 1 \cdot 10^3 + 2 \cdot 10^2 + 3 \cdot 10^1 + 4 \cdot 10^0 = 1000 + 200 + 30 + 4. \end{equation*}
