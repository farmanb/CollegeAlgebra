    Section 2.1 Circles

    Objectives

    • Complete the square to put the equation of a circle into standard form.
    • Use the standard form of a circle to identify the center and radius.
    • Use the standard form of a circle to produce a graph.
    In this section, we study circles in the Cartesian plane.

    Definition 2.1.1.

    The circle with radius \(r\) centered at \((h,k)\) consists of all points in the Cartesian plane \(r\) units away from \((h,k)\) as depicted in Figure 2.1.2.
    To find the equation of the circle with radius \(r\) and center \((h,k)\) we use The Distance Formula. A point \((x,y)\) is on this circle if the distance between \((x,y)\) and \((h,k)\) is exactly \(r\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} r = \sqrt{(x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2}. \end{equation*}
    To make this simpler, we square each side of the equation.

    Definition 2.1.3. The Standard Form of a Circle.

    The standard form of a circle with radius \(r\) and center \((h,k)\) is
    \begin{equation*} (x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2. \end{equation*}

    Definition 2.1.4.

    A diameter of a circle is a line that connects two points on the circle through the center. The length of a diameter of a circle of radius \(r\) is \(2r\text{,}\) and the midpoint of the diameter is the center of the circle.

    Subsection 2.1.1 Completing the Square

    Remember that when we multiply two binomials, we must distribute. We can remember how to distribute using the mnemonic FOIL: First, Outer, Inner, Last:
    We can use this method to multiply a binomial by itself
    \begin{align*} (x + a)^2 \amp = (x + a)(x + a)\\ \amp = xx + xa + ax + aa\\ \amp = x^2 + ax + ax + a^2\\ \amp = x^2 + 2ax + a^2. \end{align*}
    We call a trinomial of the form \(x^2 + 2ax + a^2\) a perfect square.
    If we have a trinomial of the form \(ax^2 + bx + c\text{,}\) then we can use the formula
    \begin{equation*} (x + a)^2 = x^2 + 2ax + a^2 \end{equation*}
    to complete the square as follows.

    Algorithm 2.1.7. Completing the Square.

    1. Factor an \(a\) from the first two terms to get
      \begin{equation*} ax^2 + bx + c = a\left(x^2 + \frac{b}{a}x\right) + c. \end{equation*}
    2. Add and subtract \(\left(\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2\) inside the parentheses to get
      \begin{equation*} ax^2 + bx + c = a\left(x^2 + \frac{b}{a}x + \left(\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2 \right) + c \end{equation*}
    3. Recognize the first three terms inside the parentheses as a perfect square
      \begin{equation*} ax^2 + bx + c = a\left(\left(x + \frac{b}{2a}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2 \right) + c \end{equation*}
    4. Distribute the \(a\) in front through the sum to get
      \begin{align*} ax^2 + bx + c \amp= a\left(x + \frac{b}{2a}\right)^2 - a\left(\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2 + c \end{align*}

    Example 2.1.8.

    Complete the square with \(x^2 + 4x + 5\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    When the coefficient on \(x^2\) is 1, we can skip the first step and the last step:
    \begin{align*} x^2 + 4x + 5 \amp= x^2 + 4x + 2^2 - 2^2 + 5 \amp\amp \text{Add and subtract}\ \left(\frac{4}{2}\right)^2 = 2^2 = 4\\ \amp = (x + 2)^2 - 4 + 5 \amp\amp \text{Factor}\\ \amp = (x + 2)^2 + 1 \amp\amp \text{Simplify}. \end{align*}

    Example 2.1.9.

    Complete the square with \(3x^2 - 4x + 5\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    When the coefficient on \(x\) is a negative number, such as \(x^2 - 4x + 5\text{,}\) we can still use the formula by rewriting the subtraction as addition with a negative number.
    \begin{align*} 3x^2 - 4x + 5 \amp = 3x^2 + (-4)x + 5 \amp\amp \text{Factor}\ 3\ \text{from the first two terms}\\ \amp= 3\left(x^2 + \left(-\frac{4}{3}\right)x\right) + 5 \amp\amp \text{Add and subtract}\ \left(\frac{\frac{4}{3}}{2}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^2\\ \amp = 3\left(x^2 + \left(-\frac{4}{3}\right)x + \left(-\frac{2}{3}\right)^2 - \left(-\frac{2}{3}\right)^2\right) + 5 \amp\amp \text{Factor}\\ \amp = 3\left(\left(x + \left(-\frac{2}{3}\right)\right)^2 - \frac{4}{9}\right) + 5 \amp\amp \text{Distribute}\\ \amp = 3\left(x - \frac{2}{3}\right)^2 - \frac{4}{3} + \frac{15}{3} \amp\amp \text{Simplify}\\ \amp = 3\left(x - \frac{2}{3}\right)^2 + \frac{11}{3}. \end{align*}

    Subsection 2.1.2 Recognizing Circles

    Sometimes, we will encounter the equation of a circle that is not in standard form. We can use Completing the Square twice to put it back into standard form. For example, consider the equation \(x^2 - 4x + y^2 + 6x = -9.\) While this does not look like a circle, we can first complete the square in \(x\) as follows. First, recognize \(x^2 - 4x = x^2 + (-4)x\text{.}\) Add \((-2)^2 = 4\) to both sides of the equation to obtain
    \begin{equation*} x^2 + (-4)x + (-2)^2 + y^2 + 6x = -9 + (-2)^2. \end{equation*}
    Rewrite the first three terms as a perfect square to obtain
    \begin{equation*} (x - 2)^2 + y^2 + 6x = -5. \end{equation*}
    Next, we complete the square in \(y\text{.}\) Adding \(3^2 = 9\) to both sides yields
    \begin{equation*} (x - 2)^2 + y^2 + 6x + 3^2 = -5 + 9. \end{equation*}
    Recognize the trinomial as a perfect square to find
    \begin{equation*} (x - 2)^2 + (y + 3)^2 = 4. \end{equation*}
    Finally, rewrite \(y + 3\) as \(y - (-3)\) and \(4 = 2^2\) to arrive at the standard form of the circle
    \begin{equation*} (x - 2)^2 + (y - (-3))^2 = 2^2. \end{equation*}
    Since these operations preserve the solutions to our equation, we can see that the equation \(x^2 - 4x + y^2 + 6x = -9\) represents the circle of radius \(2\) centered at the point \((2, -3)\text{.}\)
