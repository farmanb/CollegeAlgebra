    Section 1.4 The Midpoint Formula

    Objectives

    • Find the midpoint between two points in the Cartesian plane.
    Within the Cartesian plane, we will often want to determine the midpoint between two points. In this section, we extend the notion of midpoint between two points on the number line to the Cartesian plane.

    Definition 1.4.1.

    The midpoint between two numbers \(x\) and \(y\) on the number line is given by the formula
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x + y}{2}, \end{equation*}
    as depicted in Figure 1.4.2.

    Remark 1.4.3.

    The midpoint is so named because it is halfway between the numbers \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\) We can check this using Definition 1.3.1: the distance from \(x\) to the midpoint is
    \begin{align*} \abs{x - \frac{x + y}{2}} \amp = \abs{\frac{2x}{2} - \frac{x + y}{2}}\\ \amp = \abs{\frac{2x - x - y}{2}}\\ \amp = \frac{\abs{x - y}}{2} \end{align*}
    and the distance from the midpoint to \(y\) is
    \begin{align*} \abs{\frac{x + y}{2} - y} \amp = \abs{\frac{x + y}{2} - \frac{2y}{2}}\\ \amp = \abs{\frac{x + y - 2y}{2}}\\ \amp = \frac{\abs{x - y}}{2}. \end{align*}
    Similar to the distance formula, we can find the midpoint of a line segment between the points \((x_1, y_1)\) and \((x_2, y_2)\) in the Cartesian plane using a triangle. We use Definition 1.4.1 to find the midpoint of the vertical and horizontal sides of the triangle, and use those as the coordinates for the midpoint as depicted in Figure 1.4.4.

    Definition 1.4.5. The Midpoint Formula.

    The midpoint between \((x_1, y_1)\) and \((x_2, y_2)\) in the Cartesian plane is the point
    \begin{equation*} \left(\frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}\right). \end{equation*}

    Example 1.4.6.

    Use The Midpoint Formula to find the midpoint between \((1,-3)\) and \((-3, 5)\)
    Solution.
    The \(x\)-coordinate of the midpoint is
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1 + (-3)}{2} = \frac{-2}{2} = -1 \end{equation*}
    and the \(y\)-coordinate of the midpoint is
    \begin{equation*} \frac{-3 + 5}{2} = \frac{2}{2} = 1. \end{equation*}
    Therefore the midpoint is \((-1,1).\)

    Remark 1.4.7.

    Just as with the midpoint on the real line, the midpoint is halfway between the points \((x_1, y_1)\) and \((x_2, y_2)\text{.}\) The easiest way to see this is true is to make two smaller triangles inside the bigger triangle, as depicted in Figure 1.4.8.
    The two shaded triangles have the same side lengths. The horizontal side has length
    \begin{equation*} \frac{a}{2} = \frac{\abs{x_1 - x_2}}{2} \end{equation*}
    and the vertical side has length
    \begin{equation*} \frac{b}{2} = \frac{\abs{y_1 - y_2}}{2}. \end{equation*}
    Using The Pythagorean Theorem, the third side of each triangle has length
    \begin{align*} \sqrt{\left(\frac{a}{2}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{b}{2}\right)^2} \amp = \sqrt{\frac{a^2}{4} + \frac{b^2}{4}}\\ \amp = \sqrt{\frac{a^2 + b^2}{4}}\\ \amp = \frac{\sqrt{a^2 + b^2}}{\sqrt{4}}\\ \amp = \frac{\sqrt{c^2}}{2}\\ \amp = \frac{c}{2}, \end{align*}
    which is exactly half the distance between the points \((x_1, y_1)\) and \((x_2, y_2)\text{.}\)
