    Section 2.2 Measures of Change

    Functions are important tools in the application of mathematics because they allow us to formalize the relationship between two quantities. We are often interested in answering questions precisely about how the dependent variable responds to changes in the independent variable. While it is useful to be able to precisely quantify this relationship, it is often easier to understand these relationships visually. Our goal is to build a dictionary between the algebraic measures of change and the features of the graph.

    Subsection 2.2.1 Intervals of Increase and Decrease

    The terms increasing and decreasing are useful qualitative descriptions of a function’s behavior. A function is increasing if its \(y\)-values increase as its \(x\)-values increase and decreases if its \(y\)-values decrease as its \(x\)-values increase. This is easy to detect visually because either the height of the graph increases or decreases as you trace the graph from left to right.
    Unfortunately, many interesting functions are not simply increasing or decreasing. Rather, most interesting functions have pieces that are increasing and pieces that are decreasing.
    For this reason, we define increasing and decreasing on an interval to provide the flexibility to describe a range of interesting functions.

    Definition 2.2.3.

    Assume \(A \subseteq \R\) and \([a,b] \subseteq A\text{.}\) We say the function \(f \colon A \to \R\) is
    • increasing on the interval \([a,b]\) if for every \(a \leq x_1 \lt x_2 \leq b\text{,}\)
      \begin{equation*} f(x_1) \lt f(x_2). \end{equation*}
    • decreasing on the interval \([a,b]\) if for every \(a \leq x_1 \lt x_2 \leq b\text{,}\)
      \begin{equation*} f(x_2) \lt f(x_1). \end{equation*}
    Points where the graph changes between increasing and decreasing are often interesting features of the graph. These points are known collectively as local extrema.

    Definition 2.2.4.

    Assume \(A \subseteq \R\text{.}\) The function \(f \colon A \to \R\) has a local maximum at \((c, f(c))\) if there exist values \(a,b \in A\) such that
    1. \(f\) is increasing on the interval \([a,c]\text{,}\) and
    2. \(f\) is decreasing on the interval \([c,b]\text{.}\)
    We say \(f(c)\) is a local maximum value

    Definition 2.2.5.

    Assume \(A \subseteq \R\text{.}\) The function \(f \colon A \to \R\) has a local minimum at \((c, f(c))\) if there exist values \(a,b \in A\) such that
    1. \(f\) is decreasing on the interval \([a,c]\text{,}\) and
    2. \(f\) is increasing on the interval \([c,b]\text{.}\)
    We say \(f(c)\) is a local minimum value

    Example 2.2.6.

    Use the following graph to find the intervals of increase and decrease. Identify all local extrema.
    Solution.
    The graph of \(f\) is increasing on the intervals
    • \((-\infty,a]\text{,}\)
    • \([b,c]\text{,}\) and
    • \(\displaystyle [d, \infty).\)
    The graph of \(f\) is decreasing on the intervals
    • \([a,b]\text{,}\) and
    • \(\displaystyle [c,d]\)
    The points \((a, f(a))\) and \((c,f(c)\) are local maxima and the points \((b, f(b))\) and \((d, f(d))\) are local minima.

    Subsection 2.2.2 Average Rate of Change

    The rate at which \(y = f(x)\) changes as \(x\) changes is a quantitative measure of change. Suppose we have the following data about a five hour road trip.
    While we cannot pinpoint exactly how fast the car was moving at any given time, we can use this data to give a rough estimate for the speed of the car over a period of time. If we wanted to estimate the speed of the car over the two hour period from 3:00 PM until 5:00 PM, we could take the distance traveled
    \begin{equation*} 270 - 165 = 105\ \text{miles} \end{equation*}
    and divide it by the number of hours traveled to obtain
    \begin{equation*} \frac{105\ \text{miles}}{2\ \text{hours}} = 52.5\ \frac{\text{miles}}{\text{hours}}. \end{equation*}
    This represents the average speed of the car during this period of time. This idea generalizes to arbitrary functions naturally.

    Definition 2.2.9. The Average Rate of Change.

    Assume \(f\) is a function and the domain of \(f\) contains the interval \([a,b]\text{.}\) The average rate of change of \(f\) on the interval \([a,b]\) is
    \begin{equation*} \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b-a}. \end{equation*}

    Example 2.2.10.

    An object is thrown straight up in the air. The object reaches a maximum height of 1 meter after 1 seconds, and returns to the thrower’s hand after 2 seconds. The function \(h \colon [0,2] \to \R\) models the height of the object in meters as a function of \(x\) seconds.
    (a)
    What is the average rate of change for the function on the interval \([0,1]\text{?}\) Interpret the meaning of this number.
    Solution.
    Using The Average Rate of Change, the average rate of change on the interval \([0,1]\) is
    \begin{equation*} \frac{h(1) - h(0)}{1 - 0} = \frac{1 - 0}{1 - 0} = 1. \end{equation*}
    This tells us the object is moving up at an average rate of 1 meter per second.
    (b)
    What is the average rate of change for the function on the interval \([0,1]\text{?}\) Interpret the meaning of this number.
    Solution.
    Using The Average Rate of Change, the average rate of change on the interval \([0,1]\) is
    \begin{equation*} \frac{h(2) - h(1)}{2 - 1} = \frac{0 - 1}{2 - 1} = \frac{-1}{1} = -1 \end{equation*}
    This tells us the object is falling back at an average rate of 1 meter per second.
    (c)
    What is the average rate of change for the function on the interval \([0,2]\text{?}\)
    Solution.
    Using The Average Rate of Change, the average rate of change on the interval \([0,1]\) is
    \begin{equation*} \frac{f(2) - f(0)}{2 - 0} = \frac{0 - 0}{2 - 0} = 0. \end{equation*}
    We can interpret this as saying the object climbs and falls at the same speed.
