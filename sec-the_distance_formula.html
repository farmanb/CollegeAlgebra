    Section 1.4 The Distance Formula

    Within the Cartesian plane, we will often want to determine the distance between two points. In this section, we extend the notion of distance between two points on the number line to the Cartesian plane.

    Subsection 1.4.1 Distance in One Dimension

    Definition 1.4.1.

    Assume \(x\) and \(y\) are any two numbers. The distance from \(x\) to \(y\) on the number line is given by the formula
    \begin{equation*} \abs{x - y} = \sqrt{(x - y)^2} \end{equation*}
    The number \(\abs{x - y}\) measures the length of the segment of the number line starting at \(x\) and ending at \(y\text{.}\)
    Of course, we could also ask about the length of the segment of the number line starting at \(y\) and ending at \(x\text{.}\) Figure 1.4.2 suggests that these two numbers should be the same. We can verify this algebraically using Definition 1.4.1: the distance from \(y\) to \(x\) on the number line is
    \begin{align*} \abs{y - x} \amp= \sqrt{(y - x)^2} \amp\amp \text{Factor}\ -1\ \text{from}\ y - x\\ \amp= \sqrt{\left((-1)(x - y)\right)^2} \amp\amp \text{Expand}\\ \amp= \sqrt{(-1)(x - y)(-1)(x - y)} \amp\amp \text{Reorder terms}\\ \amp= \sqrt{(-1)(-1)(x - y)(x - y)} \amp\amp \text{Simplify}\\ \amp= \sqrt{(x - y)^2}\\ \amp= \abs{x - y} \end{align*}
    We say the distance is symmetric to signify that the distance between \(x\) and \(y\) whether you start measuring at \(x\) and end at \(y\text{,}\) or if you start measuring at \(y\) and end at \(x\text{.}\)

    Subsection 1.4.2 Distance in Two Dimensions

    Our goal for this section is to measure the distance between two points in the plane using Definition 1.4.1 First, we must agree on what that statement should mean. In mathematics, whenever a term might be ambiguous, we remove the ambiguity by defining the term explicitly.

    Definition 1.4.3.

    The distance between the points \((x_1,y_1)\) and \((x_2,y_2)\) in the Cartesian plane is the length of the line segment connecting the two points.
    To measure the length of this line, we need a little help from geometry. If our line segment were either horizontal or vertical, then we could use Definition 1.4.1 to measure the parallel segment of the \(x\)-axis or \(y\)-axis, respectively. To take advantage of these observations, we draw one horizontal line and one vertical line to form a right triangle.
    From Figure 1.4.5, we can see the length of the horizontal side is the distance between the points \(x_1\) and \(x_2\) along the \(x\)-axis. Similarly, the length of the vertical side is the distance between the points \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) along the \(y\)-axis. Using Definition 1.4.1, we have
    \begin{equation*} a = \abs{x_1 - x_2} = \sqrt{(x_1 - x_2)^2} \quad\text{and}\quad b = \abs{y_1 - y_2} = \sqrt{(y_1 - y_2)^2}. \end{equation*}
    Since we know two of the three sides, we can use the Pythagorean Theorem to find the third side.

    Theorem 1.4.6. The Pythagorean Theorem.

    The right triangle in Figure 1.4.7 satisfies the equation \(a^2 + b^2 = c^2\)
    Square the two side lengths
    \begin{equation*} a^2 = \sqrt{(x_1 - x_2)^2}^2 = (x_1 - x_2)^2 \quad\text{and}\quad b^2 = \sqrt{(y_1 - y_2)^2} = (y_1 - y_2)^2. \end{equation*}
    By the Pythagorean Theorem, the square of the distance between the points \((x_1, y_1)\) and \((x_2, y_2)\) satisfies
    \begin{equation*} \ell^2 = (x_1 - x_2)^2 + (y_1 - y_2)^2 \end{equation*}
    Taking the square root of both sides yields the distance formula in the plane.

    Formula 1.4.8. The Distance Formula.

    The distance from the point \((x_1,y_1)\) to the point \((x_2,y_2)\) in the Cartesian plane is given by
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt{(x_1 - x_2)^2 + (y_1 - y_2)^2} \end{equation*}

    Remark 1.4.9.

    Just like the distance between numbers, the distance between two points in the plane is symmetric. We can verify this using Formula 1.4.8:
    \begin{align*} \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2} \amp= \sqrt{\left((-1)(x_1 - x_2)\right)^2 + \left((-1)(y_1 - y_2)\right)^2}\\ \amp= \sqrt{(-1)^2(x_1 - x_2)^2 + (-1)^2(y_1 - y_2)^2}\\ \amp= \sqrt{(x_1 - x_2)^2 + (y_1 - y_2)^2} \end{align*}

    Example 1.4.10.

    Find the distance between the points \((3,-2)\) and \((4,7)\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    By The Distance Formula the distance between the points \((3,-2)\) and \((4,7)\) is
    \begin{align*} \sqrt{(3 - 4)^2 + (-2 - 7)^2} \amp= \sqrt{(-1)^2 + (-9)^2}\\ \amp = \sqrt{1 + 81}\\ \amp = \sqrt{82}. \end{align*}

    Exercises 1.4.3 Exercises

    Distance Between Points.

    Plot each pair of points in the plane. Find the length of the line segment that connects them.
    1.
    \((3,2)\) and \((-2,-10)\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    The distance between the points \((3,2)\) and \((-2,-10)\) is given by the formula
    \begin{align*} \amp \sqrt{\left(3 - (-2)\right)^2 + \left(2 - (-10)\right)^2} \amp\amp \text{Simplify double negatives}\\ \amp= \sqrt{(3 + 2)^2 + (2 + 10)^2} \amp\amp \text{Add}\\ \amp= \sqrt{5^2 + 12^2} \amp\amp \text{Square}\\ \amp= \sqrt{25 + 144} \amp\amp \text{Add}\\ \amp= \sqrt{169} \amp\amp \text{Take the square root}\\ \amp= 13 \end{align*}
    2.
    \((1,3)\) and \((4,7)\)
    Solution.
    The distance between the points \((1,3)\) and \((4,7)\) is given by the formula
    \begin{align*} \amp \sqrt{\left(1 - 4\right)^3 + \left(3 - 7\right)^3} \amp\amp \text{Subtract}\\ \amp= \sqrt{(-3)^2 + (-4)^2} \amp\amp \text{Square}\\ \amp= \sqrt{9 + 16} \amp\amp \text{Add}\\ \amp= \sqrt{25} \amp\amp \text{Take the square root}\\ \amp= 5 \end{align*}
    3.
    \((0,0)\) and \((3,4)\)
    Solution.
    The distance between the points \((0,0)\) and \((3,4)\) is given by the formula
    \begin{align*} \amp \sqrt{\left(0 - 3\right)^2 + \left(0 - 4\right)^2} \amp\amp \text{Subtract}\\ \amp= \sqrt{(-3)^2 + (-4)^2} \amp\amp \text{Square}\\ \amp= \sqrt{9 + 16} \amp\amp \text{Add}\\ \amp= \sqrt{25} \amp\amp \text{Take the square root}\\ \amp= 5 \end{align*}

    4.

    Find the perimeter of the triangle below.
    Solution.
    The perimeter of the triangle is found by summing the lengths of each side. The length of the vertical side is given by
    \begin{align*} \sqrt{(1 - 1)^2 + (4 - (-1))^2} \amp= \sqrt{0^2 + 5^2}\\ \amp= \sqrt{5^2}\\ \amp = 5 \end{align*}
    The length of the horizontal side is given by
    \begin{align*} \sqrt{(1 - 4)^2 + (-1 - (-1))^2} \amp= \sqrt{(-3)^2 + 0^2}\\ \amp= \sqrt{9}\\ \amp= 3 \end{align*}
    The length of the hypotenuse is given by
    \begin{align*} \sqrt{(1 - 4)^2 + (4 - (-1))^2} \amp= \sqrt{(-3)^2 + 5^2}\\ \amp= \sqrt{9 + 25}\\ \amp= \sqrt{34} \end{align*}
    Therefore the perimeter of the triangle is
    \begin{equation*} 5 + 3 + \sqrt{34} = 8 + \sqrt{34} \end{equation*}
