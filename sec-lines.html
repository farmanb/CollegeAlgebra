    Section 3.3 Representations of Lines

    Objectives

    • Determine the slope of a line through two points.
    • Represent a line using
      • Slope-Intercept Form,
      • Point-Slope Form, and
      • Standard Form.
    In the previous section, we encountered linear functions and saw that we can express these functions in the form \(y = mx + b\text{.}\) When we graphed a linear function, we used to points to determine the line that represents the graph. We can reverse this process to find a linear function. The first step is to determine the slope. In doing this, we will find two other representations for lines.

    Subsection 3.3.1 The Slope of a Line through two Points

    Definition 3.3.1. The Slope of a Line.

    The slope of the line through the points \((x_1, y_1)\) and \((x_2, y_2)\) is
    \begin{equation*} \frac{y_1 - y_2}{x_1 - x_2} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}, \end{equation*}
    provided that \(x_1 \neq x_2\text{.}\) The slope measures the rate of change in \(y\) with respect to \(x\text{,}\) as depicted in Figure 3.3.2.

    Remark 3.3.3.

    In the definition of The Slope of a Line, we required that \(x_1 \neq x_2\text{.}\) While there is a line that passes through two distinct points with the same \(x\)-coordinate, that line is a vertical line. Since a vertical line is not a function, the slope of a vertical line undefined.

    Example 3.3.4.

    Find the slope of the line through the points \((3,5)\) and \((6,2)\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    The slope of the line through the points \((3,5)\) and \((6,2)\) is
    \begin{equation*} \frac{2 - 5}{6 - 3} = \frac{-3}{3} = -1. \end{equation*}
    This says when the \(x\)-coordinate increases by 1, the \(y\)-coordinate decreases by 1.
    Suppose we know the slope, \(m\text{,}\) and a point \((x_0, y_0)\) that lines on the line. We can use the fact that \((x_0, y_0)\) is a Solution to an Equation to determine the \(y\)-intercept. We substitute \(x_0\) for \(x\) and \(y_0\) for \(y\) in the The Slope-Intercept Form of a Line and solve for \(b\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} y_0 \amp= mx_0 + b \amp\amp \text{Subtract}\ mx_0\ \text{from both sides}\\ b \amp = y_0 - mx_0. \end{align*}

    Theorem 3.3.5.

    The \(y\)-intercept of the line with slope \(m\) through the point \((x_0, y_0)\) is
    \begin{equation*} b = y_0 - mx_0. \end{equation*}

    Example 3.3.6.

    Find the equation of the line through the points \((3,5)\) and \((6,2)\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    In Example 3.3.4, we saw the slope of the line is \(-1\text{.}\) If we use the point \((3,5)\text{,}\) then we can find the \(y\)-intercept by solving
    \begin{align*} 5 \amp= -3 + b \amp\amp \text{Add}\ 3\ \text{to both sides}\\ b \amp= 5 + 3 = 8. \end{align*}
    This tells us the equation of the line through \((3,5)\) and \((6,2)\) is
    \begin{equation*} y = -x + 8\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We could also have used the point \((6,2)\) to find an equation of the line. If we use the point \((6,2)\text{,}\) then the \(y\)-intercept is given by solving
    \begin{align*} 2 \amp= -6 + b \amp\amp \text{Add}\ 6\ \text{to both sides}\\ b \amp= 2 + 6 = 8. \end{align*}
    We can see that no matter which point we use, we obtain the same The Slope-Intercept Form of a Line.

    Subsection 3.3.2 The Point-Slope Form of a Line

    Assume we know the slope, \(m\text{,}\) of a line and a point, \((x_0, y_0)\text{,}\) on the line. Suppose \((x,y)\) is any other point on the line. Since the slope of the line is constant, we can write
    \begin{align*} m \amp= \frac{y - y_0}{x - x_0} \amp\amp \text{Multiply both sides by}\ x - x_0\\ m(x - x_0) \amp= \left(\frac{y - y_0}{x - x_0}\right)(x - x_0) \amp\amp \text{Simplify}\\ m(x - x_0) \amp = y - y_0 \amp\amp \text{Swap sides}\\ y - y_0 \amp = m(x - x_0) \end{align*}
    to find the next form of a line.

    Theorem 3.3.7. The Point-Slope Form of a Line.

    The point-slope form of the line through the point \((x_0, y_0)\) with slope \(m\) is
    \begin{equation*} y - y_0 = m(x - x_0). \end{equation*}
    This form of a line is convenient to use when we know the slope of a line and a point on the line.

    Example 3.3.8.

    Find the The Point-Slope Form of a Line using the point \((1,2)\) and the slope \(3\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Using The Point-Slope Form of a Line, the equation of the line through \((1,2)\) with slope \(3\) is
    \begin{equation*} y - 2 = 3(x - 1). \end{equation*}

    Example 3.3.9.

    Find an equation for the line that passes through the points \((3,2)\) and \((4,3)\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    We can use the The Point-Slope Form of a Line to find the line between the points \((3,2)\) and \((4,3)\text{.}\) First compute the slope of the line
    \begin{equation*} m = \frac{3 - 2}{4 - 3} = \frac{1}{1} = 1. \end{equation*}
    Use either of the two points to find the equation
    \begin{equation*} y - 2 = x - 3 \quad\text{or}\quad y - 3 = x - 4. \end{equation*}
    While these equations may look different, we can compare them by placing them in The Slope-Intercept Form of a Line. For the first equation, add \(2\) to both sides to get
    \begin{equation*} y = x - 1 \end{equation*}
    and for the second, add \(3\) to both sides to get
    \begin{equation*} y = x - 1. \end{equation*}
    In this form, we can see that these equations represent the same line.

    Subsection 3.3.3 The Standard Equation of a Line

    So far, we have only been interested in working with lines with a well-defined slope. Sometimes, it is convenient to work with lines that may be vertical. The standard equation of a line allows us to do just that.

    Definition 3.3.10. Standard Equation of a Line.

    The standard equation of a line in the variables \(x\) and \(y\) is
    \begin{equation*} ax + by = c, \end{equation*}
    where \(a\) and \(b\) are not both zero.

    Remark 3.3.11. Caution!

    The standard equation of a line may not always represent an equation where \(y\) is a function of \(x\text{!}\) If \(b = 0\text{,}\) then the standard equation of a line reduces to
    \begin{align*} ax \amp= c \amp\amp \text{Divide both sides by}\ a\\ x \amp = \frac{c}{a}, \end{align*}
    which is a vertical line.

    Example 3.3.12.

    Place the linear equation
    \begin{equation*} x + 3y - 2x + 7 = 5 + y - 4x \end{equation*}
    into Standard Equation of a Line.
    Solution.
    The equation
    \begin{equation*} x + 3y - 2x + 7 = 5 + y - 4x \end{equation*}
    can be placed in standard form as follows. First, move the variables to the left-hand side of the equation. Subtract \(y - 4x\) from both sides
    \begin{equation*} x - 2x + 3y + 7 - (y - 4x) = 5 \end{equation*}
    and distribute the negative
    \begin{equation*} x - 2x + 3y + 7 - y + 4x = 5. \end{equation*}
    Collect like terms
    \begin{equation*} x - 2x + 4x + 3y - y + 7 = 5 \end{equation*}
    and simplify
    \begin{equation*} 3x + 2y + 7 = 5. \end{equation*}
    Finally, subtract 7 from both sides of the equation
    \begin{equation*} 3x + 4y = 5 - 7 \end{equation*}
    and simplify to arrive at the standard form
    \begin{equation*} 3x + 4y = -2. \end{equation*}

    Example 3.3.13.

    Not all equations are linear. For example, the equation \(xy + y - 7 = 2\) is not linear because of the term \(xy\text{.}\)
