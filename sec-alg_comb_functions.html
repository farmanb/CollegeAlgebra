    Section 4.1 Algebraic Combinations of Functions

    The simplest way to construct new functions from existing functions is to use the four arithmetic operations.

    Subsection 4.1.1 Sums and Differences of Functions

    Definition 4.1.1. Sum of Functions.

    Assume \(f \colon A \to \R\) and \(g \colon B \to \R\) are functions. The sum of \(f\) and \(g\) is the function \(f + g \colon A \cap B \to \R\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} (f + g)(x) = f(x) + g(x). \end{equation*}

    Remark 4.1.2.

    Suppose \(f \colon A \to \R\) and \(g \colon B \to \R\) are functions. Since addition of real numbers is commutative
    \begin{equation*} x + y = y + x, \end{equation*}
    the order of addition of functions is also commutative because
    \begin{equation*} (f + g)(x) = f(x) + g(x) = g(x) + f(x) = (g + f)(x). \end{equation*}
    That is to say \(f + g = g + f\) or, in words, the sum of \(f\) and \(g\) is the same as the sum of \(g\) and \(f\text{.}\)

    Definition 4.1.3. Difference of Functions.

    Assume \(f \colon A \to \R\) and \(g \colon B \to \R\) are functions. The difference of \(f\) and \(g\) is the function \(f - g \colon A \cap B \to \R\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} (f - g)(x) = f(x) - g(x). \end{equation*}

    Example 4.1.4.

    Consider the functions \(f \colon [0,\infty) \to \R\) defined by \(f(x) = \sqrt{x}\) and \(g \colon \R \to \R\) defined by \(g(x) = x^2\text{.}\) Find the domain of the sum of \(f\) and \(g\text{,}\) and an expression for \((f + g)(x)\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    The intersection of the domains is
    \begin{equation*} [0,\infty) \cap \R = [0,\infty) \end{equation*}
    so the sum of \(f\) and \(g\) is the function \(f + g \colon [0,\infty) \to \R\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} (f + g)(x) = f(x) + g(x) = \sqrt{x} + x^2. \end{equation*}
    In particular, we can evaluate \(f + g\) at \(x = 4\) by
    \begin{equation*} (f+g)(4) = \sqrt{4} + 4^2 = 2 + 16 = 18. \end{equation*}

    Example 4.1.5.

    Consider the functions \(f \colon [0,\infty) \to \R\) defined by \(f(x) = \sqrt{x}\) and \(g \colon \R \to \R\) defined by \(g(x) = x^2\text{.}\) Find the domain of the difference of \(f\) and \(g\text{,}\) and an expression for \((f - g)(x)\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    The intersection of the domains is
    \begin{equation*} [0,\infty) \cap \R = [0,\infty) \end{equation*}
    so the difference of \(f\) and \(g\) is the function \(f - g \colon [0,\infty) \to \R\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} (f - g)(x) = \sqrt{x} - x^2. \end{equation*}
    In particular, we can evaluate \(f - g\) at \(x = 4\) by
    \begin{equation*} (f-g)(4) = \sqrt{4} - 4^2 = 2 - 16 = -14. \end{equation*}

    Warning 4.1.6.

    Unlike the sum of functions, the difference of functions is not commutative.

    Example 4.1.7.

    Consider the functions \(f \colon [0,\infty) \to \R\) defined by \(f(x) = \sqrt{x}\) and \(g \colon \R \to \R\) defined by \(g(x) = x^2\text{.}\) Find the domain of the difference of \(g\) and \(f\text{,}\) and an expression for \((g - f)(x)\text{.}\) Conclude that \(f - g \neq g - f\) as functions.
    Solution.
    The intersection of the domains is
    \begin{equation*} [0,\infty) \cap \R = [0,\infty) \end{equation*}
    so the difference of \(g\) and \(f\) is the function \(g - f \colon [0,\infty) \to \R\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} (g - f)(x) = x^2 - \sqrt{x}. \end{equation*}
    We can see this is not the same as the function \(f - g\) because
    \begin{equation*} (g-f)(4) = 4^2 - \sqrt{4} = 16 - 2 = 14 \neq -14 = (f - g)(4). \end{equation*}

    Subsection 4.1.2 Products and Ratios of Functions

    Definition 4.1.8. Product of Functions.

    Assume \(f \colon A \to \R\) and \(g \colon B \to \R\) are functions. The product of \(f\) and \(g\) is the function \(fg \colon A \cap B \to \R\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} (fg)(x) = f(x)g(x). \end{equation*}

    Remark 4.1.9.

    Suppose \(f \colon A \to \R\) and \(g \colon A \to \R\) are functions that output real numbers. Since multiplication of real numbers is commutative
    \begin{equation*} xy = yx, \end{equation*}
    the order of multiplication of functions is also commutative because
    \begin{equation*} (fg)(x) = f(x)g(x) = g(x)f(x) = (gf)(x). \end{equation*}
    That is to say \(fg = gf\) or, in words, the product of \(f\) and \(g\) is the same as the product of \(g\) and \(f\text{.}\)

    Definition 4.1.10. Ratio of Functions.

    Assume \(f \colon A \to \R\) and \(g \colon B \to \R\) are functions, and for every \(x \in B\text{,}\) \(g(x) \neq 0\text{.}\) The ratio of \(f\) and \(g\) is the function \(f / g \colon A \cap B \to \R\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} \left(\frac{f}{g}\right)(x) = \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}. \end{equation*}

    Example 4.1.11.

    Consider the functions \(f \colon [0,\infty) \to \R\) defined by \(f(x) = \sqrt{x}\) and \(g \colon \R \to \R\) defined by \(g(x) = x^2\text{.}\) Find the domain of the product of \(f\) and \(g\text{,}\) and an expression for \((fg)(x)\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    The intersection of the domains is
    \begin{equation*} [0,\infty) \cap \R = [0,\infty) \end{equation*}
    so the product of \(f\) and \(g\) is the function \(fg \colon [0,\infty) \to \R\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} (fg)(x) = f(x)g(x) = \sqrt{x}x^2. \end{equation*}
    In particular, we can evaluate \(f g\) at \(x = 4\) by
    \begin{equation*} (fg)(4) = \sqrt{4}\left(4^2\right) = 2(16) = 32. \end{equation*}

    Example 4.1.12.

    Consider the functions \(f \colon [0,\infty) \to \R\) defined by \(f(x) = \sqrt{x}\) and \(g \colon \R \to \R\) defined by \(g(x) = x\text{.}\) Find the domain of the ratio of \(f\) and \(g\text{,}\) and an expression for \((f/g)(x)\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    The function \(g(x)\) is non-zero on the set
    \begin{equation*} \left\{x \in \R \;\middle\vert\; x \neq 0\right\} = (-\infty,0) \cup (0, \infty). \end{equation*}
    The intersection with the domain of \(f\) is the set of positive real numbers
    \begin{equation*} [0,\infty) \cap (-\infty,0) \cup (0,\infty) = (0,\infty) = \left\{x \in \R \;\middle\vert\; x > 0\right\} \end{equation*}
    so the ratio of \(f\) and \(g\) is the function \(f / g \colon (0,\infty) \to \R\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} \left(\frac{f}{g}\right)(x) = \frac{\sqrt{x}}{x}. \end{equation*}
    In particular, we can evaluate \(f / g\) at \(x = 4\) by
    \begin{equation*} \left(\frac{f}{g}\right)(4) = \frac{\sqrt{4}}{4} = \frac{2}{4} = \frac{1}{2}. \end{equation*}

    Warning 4.1.13.

    Unlike the sum and product of functions, the ratio of functions is not commutative.

    Example 4.1.14.

    Consider the functions \(f \colon [0,\infty) \to \R\) defined by \(f(x) = \sqrt{x}\) and \(g \colon \R \to \R\) defined by \(g(x) = x\text{.}\) Find the domain of the ratio of \(f\) and \(g\text{,}\) and an expression for \((f/g)(x)\text{.}\) Conclude that \(f/g \neq g/f\) as functions.
    Solution.
    The function \(f(x)\) is non-zero on the set \((0, \infty)\text{.}\) The intersection with the domain of \(g\) is the set of positive real numbers
    \begin{equation*} (0,\infty) \cap \R = (0,\infty) = \left\{x \in \R \;\middle\vert\; x > 0\right\} \end{equation*}
    so the ratio of \(g\) and \(f\) is the function \(g / f \colon (0,\infty) \to \R\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} \left(\frac{f}{g}\right)(x) = \frac{x}{\sqrt{x}}. \end{equation*}
    In particular, we can evaluate \(g / f\) at \(x = 4\) to see
    \begin{equation*} \left(\frac{g}{f}\right)(4) = \frac{4}{\sqrt{4}} = \frac{4}{2} = 2 \neq \frac{1}{2} = \left(\frac{f}{g}\right)(4). \end{equation*}

    Subsection 4.1.3 Rational Functions

    A special case of ratios of functions arise when both the numerator and denominator are polynomials. These functions are of significant importance in many areas of mathematics, as well as its applications.

    Definition 4.1.15.

    A rational function is a function of the form
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \left(\frac{p}{q}\right)(x) = \frac{p(x)}{q(x)}, \end{equation*}
    where \(p\) and \(q \neq 0\) are polynomial functions.
    Since polynomial functions always have domain \(\R\text{,}\) the domain of the rational function \(f = p/q\) is the set
    \begin{equation*} D = \left\{x \in \R \;\middle\vert\; q(x) \neq 0\right\}. \end{equation*}

    Example 4.1.16.

    Find the domain of the rational function
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \frac{x - 2}{x^2-9} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    This expression is undefined whenever
    \begin{equation*} x^2 - 9 = (x - 3)(x + 3) = 0 \end{equation*}
    Since the only solutions to this equation are \(x = 3\) and \(x = -3\text{,}\) the domain of \(f\) is
    \begin{equation*} D = \left\{x \in \R \;\middle\vert\; x \neq \pm 3\right\}. \end{equation*}
    This is the set of all numbers except \(3\) and \(-3\text{,}\) which we can express as the union of intervals
    \begin{equation*} (-\infty,-3) \cup (-3, 3) \cup (3, \infty). \end{equation*}

    Subsection 4.1.4 Simplifying Rational Functions

    Recall that we always write rational numbers in lowest terms. For example, instead of writing \(2/4\text{,}\) we cancel the common factor of \(2\) in the numerator and denominator to write \(1/2\text{.}\) The same is true for rational functions, although some extra care is required.

    Example 4.1.17.

    Simplify the function \(f \colon (-\infty, 3) \cup (3, \infty) \to \R\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \frac{x^2-9}{x - 3} \end{equation*}
    in lowest terms. What is the domain of this simplified function?
    Solution.
    To simplify the expression, factor the numerator as \(x^2 - 9 = (x - 3)(x + 3)\) and cancel the common factors in the numerator and denominator
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \frac{x - 3}{x - 3}(x + 3) = x + 3. \end{equation*}
    It is important to remember that this simplification only holds for elements of the domain of the function \(f\text{!}\) This says that \(f\) is equal to the function \(g \colon (-\infty, 3) \cup (3, \infty) \to \R\) defined by \(g(x) = x + 3\text{.}\) However, the function \(h \colon \R \to \R\) defined by \(h(x) = x + 3\) is not equal to the function \(f\) because the functions \(f\) and \(h\) do not have the same domain.

    Subsection 4.1.5 Sums and Differences of Rational Functions

    Recall that in order to add or subtract rational numbers, we must find a common denominator. For example, to add \(2/3\) and \(3/2\text{,}\) we use the common denominator \(2 \times 3 = 6\)
    \begin{align*} \frac{2}{3} + \frac{3}{2} \amp= \frac{2}{2} \times \frac{2}{3} + \frac{3}{3} \times \frac{3}{2}\\ \amp= \frac{4}{6} + \frac{9}{6}\\ \amp= \frac{4 + 9}{6}\\ \amp= \frac{13}{6}. \end{align*}
    The same is true for rational functions, provided we are careful with the denominator.

    Example 4.1.18.

    Find the sum of the rational functions
    \begin{equation*} f \colon \left\{x \in \R \;\middle\vert\; x \neq 0,\, 1\right\} \to \R \quad\text{and}\quad g \colon \left\{x \in \R \;\middle\vert\; x \neq 0,\, 2\right\} \to \R \end{equation*}
    defined by
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \frac{x + 1}{x(x-1)} \quad\text{and}\quad g(x) = \frac{x - 4}{x(x-2)}. \end{equation*}
    What is the domain of this function?
    Solution.
    To add these functions, we must first find a common denominator. We use the least common multiple of \(x(x-1)\) and \(x(x-2)\text{,}\) which is
    \begin{equation*} x(x - 1)(x - 2). \end{equation*}
    Once we have the common denominator, the process of adding rational functions is nearly the same as for rational numbers:
    \begin{align*} \frac{x + 1}{x(x-1)} + \frac{x - 4}{x(x-2)} \amp = \frac{x-2}{x-2}\left(\frac{x + 1}{x(x-1)}\right) + \frac{x-1}{x-1}\left(\frac{x - 4}{x(x-2)}\right)\\ \amp=\frac{(x-2)(x+1)}{x(x-1)(x-2)} + \frac{(x-1)(x-4)}{x(x-1)(x-2)}\\ \amp=\frac{(x-2)(x+1) + (x-1)(x-4)}{x(x-1)(x-2)}\\ \amp=\frac{x^2 +x - 2x - 2 + x^2 - 4x -x + 4}{x(x-1)(x-2)}\\ \amp=\frac{2x^2 + 1x - 2x -4x -1x -2 + 4}{x(x-1)(x-2)}\\ \amp=\frac{2x^2 -6x + 2}{x(x-1)(x-2)}. \end{align*}
    By Definition 4.1.1, the function \(f + g\) is only defined where both of \(f\) and \(g\) are defined. Therefore the sum of \(f\) and \(g\) is the function
    \begin{equation*} f + g \colon \left\{x \in \R \;\middle\vert\; x \neq 0,\,1,\,2\right\} \to \R \end{equation*}
    defined by
    \begin{equation*} (f + g)(x) = \frac{2x^2 -6x + 2}{x(x-1)(x-2)}. \end{equation*}

    Subsection 4.1.6 Products and Ratios of Rational Functions

    Recall that when we multiply two rational numbers we multiply the numerators and denominators. For example,
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{3}{4}\right) = \frac{1(3)}{2(4)} = \frac{3}{8}. \end{equation*}
    When we divide one rational number by another, we multiply the one by the reciprocal of the other. For example
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\frac{1}{2}}{\frac{3}{4}} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{4}{3}\right) = \frac{4}{6} = \frac{2}{3}. \end{equation*}
    The same is true for products and ratios of rational functions.

    Example 4.1.19.

    Find the product of the rational functions
    \begin{equation*} f \colon \left\{x \in \R \;\middle\vert\; x \neq 0,\, 1\right\} \to \R \quad\text{and}\quad g \colon \left\{x \in \R \;\middle\vert\; x \neq 0,\, 2\right\} \to \R \end{equation*}
    defined by
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \frac{x + 1}{x(x-1)} \quad\text{and}\quad g(x) = \frac{x - 4}{x(x-2)}. \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    We multiply the rational functions by multiplying the numerators and the denominators
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x + 1}{x(x-1)}\left(\frac{x - 4}{x(x-2)}\right) = \frac{(x+1)(x-4)}{x^2(x-1)(x-2)} \end{equation*}
    By Definition 4.1.8, the function \(fg\) is only defined where both \(f\) and \(g\) are defined. Therefore product of \(f\) and \(g\) is the function
    \begin{equation*} fg \colon \left\{x \in \R \;\middle\vert\; x \neq 0, 1, 2\right\} \to \R \end{equation*}
    defined by
    \begin{equation*} (fg)(x) = \frac{(x+1)(x-4)}{x^2(x-1)(x-2)}. \end{equation*}

    Example 4.1.20.

    Find the ratio of the rational functions
    \begin{equation*} f \colon \left\{x \in \R \;\middle\vert\; x \neq 0,\, 1\right\} \to \R \quad\text{and}\quad g \colon \left\{x \in \R \;\middle\vert\; x \neq 0,\, 2\right\} \to \R \end{equation*}
    defined by
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \frac{x + 1}{x(x-1)} \quad\text{and}\quad g(x) = \frac{x - 4}{x(x-2)}. \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    We can divide these rational functions by multiplying \(f\) by the reciprocal of \(g\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} \frac{\frac{x + 1}{x(x-1)}}{\frac{x - 4}{x(x-2)}} \amp= \frac{x + 1}{x(x-1)}\left(\frac{x(x-2)}{x - 4}\right)\\ \amp= \frac{x(x+1)(x-2)}{x(x-1)(x-4)}\\ \amp= \frac{(x+1)(x-2)}{(x-1)(x-4)}. \end{align*}
    By Definition 4.1.10, the function \(f/g\) is only defined where both \(f\) and \(g\) are defined and \(g\) is non-zero. Therefore \(f/g\) is the function
    \begin{equation*} fg \colon \left\{x \in \R \;\middle\vert\; x \neq 0, 1, 2,4\right\} \to \R \end{equation*}
    defined by
    \begin{equation*} \left(\frac{f}{g}\right)(x) = \frac{(x+1)(x-2)}{(x-1)(x-4)}. \end{equation*}

    Subsection 4.1.7 Rational Equations

    Just as with polynomials, we may wish to solve equations involving rational functions. Fortunately, with some care, rational equations quickly reduce to polynomial equations by simply clearing denominators.

    Example 4.1.21.

    Solve the equation
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2 - 1}{x^2 - 4} = 3 \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    First we note the domain of the rational function \((x^2 - 1)/(x^2 -4)\) is the set
    \begin{equation*} D = \left\{x \in \R \;\middle\vert\; x \neq \pm 2\right\} \end{equation*}
    As long as \(x \in D\text{,}\) we may multiply both sides of the equation by the non-zero quantity \(x^2 - 4\) to obtain the polynomial equation
    \begin{align*} \amp x^2 - 1 = 3(x^2 - 4) \amp\amp \text{Distribute}\\ \amp x^2 - 1 = 3x^2 - 12 \amp\amp \text{Add}\ 12 - x^2\ \text{to both sides}\\ \amp 11 = 2x^2 \amp\amp \text{Rewrite}\\ \amp 2x^2 = 11 \amp\amp \text{Divide both sides by}\ 2\\ \amp x^2 = \frac{11}{2} \amp\amp \text{Take the square root of each side.}\\ \amp x = \pm \sqrt{\frac{11}{2}} \end{align*}
    Therefore the two solutions are \(x = \sqrt{11/2}\) and \(x = -\sqrt{11/2}.\)
