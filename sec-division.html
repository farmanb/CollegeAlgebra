    Section 3.2 Polynomial Division

    Another method for finding roots that is often paired with The Rational Root Theorem is polynomial division. Besides finding roots, the method of polynomial division will be important in Section 5.2.
    Recall that if we have an integer \(n\) and a smaller integer, \(d\text{,}\) then we can always find integers \(q\) and \(r\) with \(r \lt d\) such that
    \begin{equation*} n = qd + r. \end{equation*}
    Long division provides a convenient method for finding \(q\) and \(r\text{.}\)

    Example 3.2.1.

    To divide \(6935\) by \(30\text{,}\) we would perform the long division using the symbols
    \begin{align*} \amp\phantom{)}231\\ 30 \amp\overline{\smash{)}{6935}} \\ \amp \underline{-60}\\ \amp \phantom{lllll}93\\ \amp \phantom{lll}\underline{-90}\\ \amp \phantom{lllllll}35\\ \amp \phantom{llll}\underline{-30}\\ \amp \phantom{lllllllll}5 \end{align*}
    to find \(6935 = 30 \times 231 + 5\text{.}\)
    For polynomials, the form is nearly identical, with numbers replaced by polynomials.

    Theorem 3.2.2. Polynomial Division.

    Assume \(f\) and \(d\) are polynomials with \(\deg{d} \lt \deg{f}\text{.}\) There exist polynomials \(q\) and \(r\) with \(\deg{r} \lt \deg{d}\) such that
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = q(x)d(x) + r(x)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    In analogy with the integers, we call the polynomials \(d\) the dividend, \(q\) the quotient, and \(r\) the remainder, and we say that \(f\) divided by \(d\) is \(q\) with remainder \(r\)
    Recall for integers \(n\) and \(d\text{,}\) we say that \(d\) divides \(n\) (or \(d\) is a divisor of \(n\text{,}\) or \(d\) is a factor of \(n\)) if the remainder is zero \(n\) is divided by \(d\text{.}\) We use this same language for polynomials.

    Definition 3.2.3.

    Assume \(f\) and \(d\) are polynomials. The polynomial \(d\) divides the polynomial \(f\) if the remainder when \(f\) is divided by \(d\) is the zero polynomial, \(r(x) = 0\text{.}\)
    To understand how to perform division with polynomials, it is helpful to understand the division algorithm with numbers written in base \(10\text{.}\) The four steps in the long division above are shorthand for the following equations.
    \begin{align} 6935 \amp= 30 \times 200 + 935\tag{3.2.1}\\ 935 \amp= 30 \times 30 + 35\tag{3.2.2}\\ 35 \amp= 30 \times 1 + 5\tag{3.2.3} \end{align}
    We arrive at the final answer by repeated substitution for the remainders starting with (3.2.1).
    \begin{align*} 6935 \amp= 30 \times 200 + 935 \amp\amp \text{Substitute the RHS of}\ \knowl{./knowl/xref/eq-division_2.html}{\text{(3.2.2)}}\ \text{for}\ 935\\ \amp = 30 \times 200 + 30 \times 30 + 35 \amp\amp \text{Substitute the RHS of}\ \knowl{./knowl/xref/eq-division_3.html}{\text{(3.2.3)}}\ \text{for}\ 35\\ \amp = 30 \times 200 + 30 \times 30 + 30 \times 1 + 5 \amp\amp \text{Factor}\ 3\ \text{from the first three terms}\\ \amp= (200 + 30 + 1) \times 3 + 2 \amp\amp \text{Simplify}\\ \amp = 231 \times 30 + 5. \end{align*}

    Example 3.2.4.

    Long division with integers terminates because we reduce the number of digits in the remainder by at least one at each stage. Our goal will be similar for long division with polynomials, but we will reduce the degree of the remainder polynomial by at least one at every stage. The process terminates when the degree of the remainder is smaller than the degree of the divisor.
    Divide the polynomial \(f(x) = 6x^3 + 9x^2 + 3x + 5\) by the polynomial \(d(x) = 3x\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Write
    \begin{align} 6x^3 + 9x^2 + 3x + 5 \amp= 2x^2 \times 3x + 9x^2 + 3x + 5\tag{3.2.4}\\ 9x^2 + 3x + 5 \amp= 3x \times 3x + 3x + 5\tag{3.2.5}\\ 3x + 5 \amp= 1 \times 3x + 5.\tag{3.2.6} \end{align}
    Starting from (3.2.4), substitute for the remainders
    \begin{align*} 6x^3 + 9x^2 + 3x + 5 \amp = 2x^2 \times 3x + 9x^2 + 3x + 5 \amp\amp \text{Substitute}\ \knowl{./knowl/xref/eq-poly_div_2.html}{\text{(3.2.5)}}\\ \amp= 2x^2 \times 3x + 3x \times 3x + 3x + 5 \amp\amp \text{Substitute}\knowl{./knowl/xref/eq-poly_div_3.html}{\text{(3.2.6)}}\\ \amp= 2x^2 \times 3x + 3x \times 3x + 1 \times 3x + 5 \amp\amp \text{Factor}\\ \amp= (2x^2 + 3x + 1) \times 3x + 5. \end{align*}
    Therefore \(f(x) = 6x^3 + 9x^2 + 3x + 5\) divided by \(d(x) = 3x\) is \(q(x) = 2x^2 + 3x + 1\) with remainder \(r(x) = 5\text{.}\)
    We can also perform polynomial division using the same shorthand as we would for numbers. When performing polynomial division in this way, it is important to carefully distribute signs when subtracting.
    \begin{align*} \amp\phantom{)}2x^2 + 3x + 1\\ 3x \amp\overline{\smash{)}{6x^3 + 9x^2 + 3x + 5}} \\ \amp \underline{-6x^3}\\ \amp \phantom{lllll}0 + 9x^2\\ \amp \phantom{lllllllll}\underline{-9x^2}\\ \amp \phantom{6x^3 + 9x^2}0 + 3x\\ \amp \phantom{6x^3 + 9x^2 0l}\underline{- 3x}\\ \amp \phantom{6x^3 + 9x^2 + 3}0 + 5 \end{align*}

    Remark 3.2.5.

    Note that if we evaluate each of the polynomials at \(x = 10\text{,}\) then we find
    \begin{equation*} f(10) = 6000 + 900 + 30 + 5 = 6935 \end{equation*}
    divided by \(d(10) = 30\) is
    \begin{equation*} q(10) = 200 + 30 + 1 = 231 \end{equation*}
    with remainder \(r(10) = 5\text{.}\) Indeed, polynomial long division is a generalization of long division in base \(10\text{.}\)
    When paired with The Rational Root Theorem, polynomial long division is useful for factoring polynomials. The main tool is the following theorem.

    Theorem 3.2.6.

    The number \(r\) is a root of the polynomial \(f\) if and only if \((x - r)\) divides \(f\text{.}\)

    Example 3.2.7.

    As we saw in Example 3.1.14, the polynomial \(f(x) = 2x^4 - 7x^3 + 5x^2 - 7x + 3\) has a root at \(x = 1/2\) and at \(x = 3\text{.}\) Use polynomial division to factor \(f\) completely.
    Solution.
    By Theorem 3.2.6, the polynomial \(x - 1/2\) divides \(f(x)\)
    \begin{align*} \amp\phantom{)}2x^3 - 6x^2 + 2x - 6\\ x - \frac{1}{2} \amp\overline{\smash{)}{2x^4 - 7x^3 + 5x^2 - 7x + 3}} \\ \amp \underline{-2x^4 + x^3}\\ \amp \phantom{)2x^4}-6x^3 + 5x^2\\ \amp \phantom{)2x^4}\underline{+6x^3 - 3x^2}\\ \amp \phantom{)2x^4 - 7x^3 +}2x^2 - 7x\\ \amp \phantom{)2x^4 - 7x^3}\underline{-2x^2 + x}\\ \amp \phantom{)2x^4 - 7x^3 + 5x^2}-6x + 3\\ \amp \phantom{)2x^4 - 7x^3 + 5x^2}\underline{+6x - 3}\\ \amp \phantom{)2x^4 - 7x^3 + 5x^2 - 7x +}0 \end{align*}
    This allows us to write
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \left(x - \frac{1}{2}\right)\left(2x^3 - 6x^2 + 2x - 6\right). \end{equation*}
    Since \(f(3) = 0\) and \(3 - 1/2 \neq 0\text{,}\) we conclude that \(x - 3\) divides \(2x^3 - 6x^2 + 2x - 6\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} \amp\phantom{)}2x^2 + 2\\ x - 3 \amp\overline{\smash{)}{2x^3 - 6x^2 + 2x - 6}} \\ \amp \underline{-2x^3 + 6x^2}\\ \amp \phantom{)2x^3}0 + 2x - 6\\ \amp \phantom{)2x^3-}\underline{-2x + 6}\\ \amp \phantom{)2x^3 - 6x^2 + }0 \end{align*}
    This provides the factorization
    \begin{align*} f(x) \amp= \left(x - \frac{1}{2}\right)(x - 3)(2x^2 + 2)\\ \amp = \left(x - \frac{1}{2}\right)(x - 3)(2)(x^2 + 1)\\ \amp = 2\left(x - \frac{1}{2}\right)(x - 3)(x^2 + 1)\\ \amp = (2x - 1)(x - 3)(x-i)(x+i). \end{align*}

    Exercises Exercises

    1.

    Use the polynomial \(f(x) = x^3 - 3x^2 + 2x\) to answer the following questions.
    (a)
    Use the The Rational Root Theorem to list all possible rational roots of \(f\text{.}\)
    (b)
    Use your list to find all the roots of \(f\text{.}\)
    (c)
    Use the roots of \(f\) to find its factorization.

    2.

    Use the polynomial \(f(x) = x^3 - x^2 - 2x + 2\) to answer the following questions.
    (a)
    Use the The Rational Root Theorem to list all possible rational roots of \(f\text{.}\)
    (b)
    Use your list to find all rational roots of \(f\text{.}\)
    (c)
    Using the rational roots, use polynomial division to factor \(f\) as the product of a degree \(1\) polynomial and a degree \(2\) polynomial.
    (d)
    What can you say about the other roots of \(f\text{?}\)
