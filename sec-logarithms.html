    Section 5.2 The Logarithm

    Assume \(a \neq 1\) is a positive real number. The graph of \(\Exp{a} \colon \R \to (0,\infty)\) satisfies The Horizontal Line Test.
    We can verify this algebraically as follows. Suppose the points \(\left(x_1, a^{x_1}\right)\) and \(\left(x_2, a^{x_2}\right)\) both lie on the same horizontal line. Since they lie on a horizontal line, they must have the same \(y\)-coordinates and so
    \begin{align*} a^{x_1} \amp = a^{x_2} \amp\amp \text{Divide both sides by}\ a^{x_2}\\ \frac{a^{x_1}}{a^{x_2}} \amp = 1 \amp\amp\text{Combine exponents}\\ a^{x_1 - x_2} \amp= 1 \end{align*}
    implies that \(x_1 - x_2 = 0\) and so \(x_1 = x_2\text{.}\) That is to say, the two points are the same point and so any horizontal line intersects the graph in at most one point. This tells us the exponential function is invertible.

    Definition 5.2.2. The Logarithm with Base \(a\).

    Assume \(a \neq 1\) is a positive real number. The logarithm with base \(a\), \(\log_a \colon (0,\infty) \to \R\text{,}\) is the Composition Inverse of \(\operatorname{Exp}{a} \colon \R \to (0, \infty)\text{.}\)
    In particular, for every real number \(x\) and every positive real number \(y\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \log_a \circ \Exp{a}(x) = \log_a(a^x) = x \quad\text{and}\quad \Exp{a} \circ \log_a(y) = a^{\log_a(y)} = y. \end{equation*}

    Definition 5.2.3. The Natural Logarithm.

    The natural logarithm, \(\ln \colon (0, \infty) \to \R\text{,}\) is the logarithm with base \(e\text{.}\)

    Subsection 5.2.1 Properties of the Logarithm

    Assume \(a \neq 1\) is a positive real number. The properties of the logarithm are all consequences of the Properties of Exponential Functions and Theorem 4.3.10.
    The first property of the logarithm is that \(\log_a(1) = 0\text{.}\) Since \(\Exp{a}(0) = a^0 = 1\text{,}\) we can write
    \begin{equation*} \log_a(1) = \log_a(a^0) = 0. \end{equation*}
    Just as the graph of every exponential function passes through the point \((0,1)\text{,}\) the graph of every logarithm passes through the point \((1,0)\text{.}\)
    For the remaining properties, recall that the exponential function turns addition into multiplication and subtraction into division:
    \begin{equation*} \Exp{a}(x + x^\prime) = \Exp{a}(x)\Exp{a}(x^\prime) \quad\text{and}\quad \Exp{a}(x - x^\prime) = \frac{\Exp{a}(x)}{\Exp{a}\left(x^\prime\right)}. \end{equation*}
    As the composition inverse of \(\Exp{a}\text{,}\) \(\log_a\) inverse this relationship by turning multiplication into addition and division into subtraction.
    To see this, assume \(y\) and \(y^\prime\) are positive real numbers. Let \(x = \log_a(y)\) and \(x^\prime = \log_a\left(y^\prime\right)\text{.}\) Observe that
    \begin{equation*} \Exp{a}(x) = \Exp{a} \circ \log_a(y) = y \quad\text{and}\quad \Exp{a}\left(x^\prime\right) = \Exp{a} \circ \log_a\left(y^\prime\right) = y^\prime. \end{equation*}
    because \(\Exp{a}\) and \(\log_a\) are composition inverses. This allows us to transform the logarithm of their product into the sum of their logarithms
    \begin{align*} \log_a(yy^\prime) \amp= \log_a\left(\Exp{a}\left(x\right)\Exp{a}\left(x^\prime\right)\right)\\ \amp= \log_a \circ \Exp{a}(x + x^\prime)\\ \amp= x + x^\prime\\ \amp= \log_a\left(y\right) + \log_a\left(y^\prime\right) \end{align*}
    and the logarithm of their ratio into the difference of their logarithms
    \begin{align*} \log_a\left(\frac{y}{y^\prime}\right) \amp= \log_a\left(\frac{\Exp{a}(x)}{\Exp{a}\left(x^\prime\right)}\right)\\ \amp=\log_a \circ \Exp{a}\left(x - x^\prime\right)\\ \amp= x - x^\prime\\ \amp= \log_a(y) - \log_a\left(y^\prime\right). \end{align*}
    The final property is a consequence of these two. While it does not provide anything new, it is frequently useful. If \(y\) is any positive real number and \(r\) is any real number, then we think of \(y^r\) as the product of \(r\) copies of the number \(y\)
    \begin{equation*} y^r = \underbrace{y \cdot y \cdot y \cdots y}_r. \end{equation*}
    If we transform the log of the product into the sum of the logs \(r\) times, then we obtain the expression
    \begin{align*} \log_a(y^r) \amp= \log_a(y) + \log_a(y^{r-1})\\ \amp= \log_a(y) + \log_a(y) + \log_a(y^{r-2})\\ \amp= \cdots\\ \amp= \underbrace{\log_a(y) + \log_a(y) + \cdots + \log_a(y)}_r\\ \amp= r\log_a(y). \end{align*}

    Theorem 5.2.4. Properties of the Logarithm.

    \(a \neq 1\)
    1. \(\displaystyle \log_a(1) = 0.\)
    2. For all positive real numbers \(y\) and \(y^\prime\text{,}\)
      \begin{equation*} \log_a(yy^\prime) = \log_a(y) + \log_a(y^\prime). \end{equation*}
    3. For all positive real numbers \(y\) and \(y^\prime\)
      \begin{equation*} \log_a\left(\frac{y}{y^\prime}\right) = \log_a(y) - \log_a(y^\prime). \end{equation*}
    4. For all positive real numbers \(y\) and all real numbers \(r\text{,}\)
      \begin{equation*} \log_a(y^r) = r\log_a(y). \end{equation*}

    Subsection 5.2.2 The Change of Base Formulas

    While inverse functions have many nice properties, it is often difficult to perform computations with them. For example, we can compute
    \begin{equation*} \log_2(64) = \log_2(2^6) \log_2 \circ \Exp{2}(6) = 6 \end{equation*}
    using the properties of inverse functions. Unfortunately, none of the properties we have discussed help us to compute \(\log_2(5)\text{.}\) At best, we can observe that
    \begin{equation*} 2^2 = 4 \lt 5 \lt 9 = 2^3 \end{equation*}
    tells us \(2 \lt \log_2(5) \lt 3\text{.}\) If we need a better approximation to the value of \(\log_2(5)\text{,}\) then we will need the assistance of a computer.
    On a modern computational tool like Desmos 1 , the underscore symbol _ can be used to enter the command log_2(5) and the result is
    \begin{equation*} \log_2(5) \approx 2.32192809489. \end{equation*}
    However, on most scientific and graphing calculators, there are only buttons labelled log and ln for the logarithms with base \(10\) and base \(e\text{,}\) respectively.
    Fortunately, we can use the properties of the logarithm to rewrite a logarithm with base \(a\) as a logarithm with a different base, \(b\text{.}\) Assume \(y\) is a positive real number. Let \(x = \log_a(y)\) so that \(y = a^x\text{.}\) First, rewrite
    \begin{equation*} a = \exp_b \circ \log_b(a) = b^{\log_b(a)} \end{equation*}
    so we have
    \begin{align*} \amp y = a^x = \left(b^{\log_b(a)}\right)^x = b^{x\log_b(a)} \amp\amp \text{Apply}\ \log_b\ \text{to both sides}\\ \amp \log_b(y) = x\log_b(a) \amp\amp \text{Rewrite}\ x = \log_a(y)\\ \amp \log_b(y) = \log_a(y)\log_b(a) \amp\amp\text{Divide both sides by}\ \log_b(a)\\ \amp \log_a(y) = \frac{\log_b(y)}{\log_b(a)}. \end{align*}
    This gives us a way to convert the logarithm with base \(a\) to a logarithm with base \(b\text{.}\)

    Formula 5.2.5. Base Change for Logarithms.

    Assume \(a \neq 1\) and \(b \neq 1\) are positive real numbers. For all positive real numbers \(y\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \log_a(y) = \frac{\log_b(y)}{\log_b(a)}. \end{equation*}

    Example 5.2.6.

    To compute \(\log_2(5)\) on a calculator, compute
    \begin{equation*} \log_2(5) = \frac{\log(5)}{\log(2)} \approx 2.32192809489 \end{equation*}
    or
    \begin{equation*} \log_2(5) = \frac{\ln(5)}{\ln(2)} \approx 2.32192809489 \end{equation*}
    In some sense, this justifies calling \(\exp(x) = e^x\) the exponential function. For any positive number \(a \neq 1\text{,}\) we can write
    \begin{equation*} \Exp{a}(x) = a^x = \left(e^{\ln(a)}\right)^x = e^{\ln(a)x} = \exp(\ln(a)x) \end{equation*}
    which allows us to regard the function \(\Exp{a}\) as a horizontal scaling of the function \(\exp\) by \(\ln(a)\text{.}\) Similarly, we can write
    \begin{equation*} \log_a(y) = \frac{\ln(y)}{\ln(a)} \end{equation*}
    so the function \(\log_a\) is a vertical scaling of the function \(\ln\text{.}\)

    Subsection 5.2.3 Graphs of Logarithms

    Since \(\log_a \colon (0, \infty) \to \R\) is the composition of \(\Exp{a} \colon \R \to (0, \infty)\text{,}\) the graph of \(y = \log_a(x)\) is the reflection of the graph of \(y = \Exp{a}(x)\) across the line \(y = x\text{.}\) Just as for exponentials, the logarithms have the same basic shape depending on whether \(1 \lt a\) or \(a \lt 1\text{.}\) We have plotted the reflections across the \(y\)-axis of the exponential growth functions in Figure 5.1.10 below.
    For any positive real number \(a \neq 1\text{,}\) we can relate the graph of \(y = \log_a(x)\) to the graph of \(y = \log_{1/a}(x)\) using the Base Change for Logarithms. Write \(1/a = a^{-1}\) so that for all positive numbers \(x\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \log_{a^{-1}}(x) = \frac{\log_a(x)}{\log_a(a^{-1})} = \frac{\log_a(x)}{-1} = -\log_a(x). \end{equation*}
    This tells us the graph of \(y = \log_a(x)\) is the reflection of \(y = \log_{a^{-1}}(x)\) across the \(x\)-axis.

    Example 5.2.8.

    Consider the logarithm with base \(1/2\text{.}\) For all positive real numbers \(y\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \log_{\frac{1}{2}}(y) = \frac{\log_2(y)}{\log_2\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)} = \frac{\log_2(y)}{-1} = -\log_2(y) \end{equation*}
    by the Base Change for Logarithms. This tells us that we can graph \(\log_{\frac{1}{2}}(y)\) by reflecting the graph of \(\log_2(y)\) across the \(x\)-axis.
