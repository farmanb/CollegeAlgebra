    Section 2.4 Parallel and Perpendicular Lines

    Objectives

    • Determine whether two lines are parallel, perpendicular, or neither.
    • Find the equation of a line parallel to a given line.
    • Find the equation of a line perpendicular to a given line.

    Definition 2.4.1. Parallel.

    Two lines in the Cartesian plane are said to be parallel if they do not intersect.

    Theorem 2.4.3.

    Two lines are parallel if and only if they have the same slope.

    Example 2.4.4.

    Determine whether the lines
    \begin{equation*} -x + 3y=-4 \quad\text{and}\quad -4x + 12y = 9 \end{equation*}
    are parallel.
    Solution.
    In order to determine whether these lines are parallel, we need to find the slope of each line. We do this by placing each line into The Slope-Intercept Form of a Line.
    For the first line, add \(x\) to both sides
    \begin{equation*} 3y = x - 4 \end{equation*}
    and divide both sides by \(3\)
    \begin{equation*} y = \frac{1}{3} - \frac{4}{3}. \end{equation*}
    This tells us the slope of the line \(-x + 3y = -4\) is \(1/3\text{.}\)
    For the second line, add \(4x\) to both sides
    \begin{equation*} 12y = 4x + 9, \end{equation*}
    divide both sides by \(12\)
    \begin{equation*} y = \frac{4}{12}x + \frac{9}{12} \end{equation*}
    and reduce
    \begin{equation*} y = \frac{1}{3}x + \frac{3}{4}. \end{equation*}
    This tells the slope of the line \(-4x + 12y = 9\) is also \(1/3\text{.}\) Therefore the lines \(-x + 3y = -4\) and \(-4x + 12y = 9\) are parallel by Theorem 2.4.3.

    Example 2.4.5.

    Find the equation of the line parallel to \(4x + 2y = 9\) that passes through the point \((-1,5)\text{.}\) Write your answer in Definition 2.3.4
    Solution.
    First, find the slope of the line \(4x + 2y = 9\) by placing the equation into The Slope-Intercept Form of a Line. Subtract \(4x\) from both sides
    \begin{equation*} 2y = -4x + 9, \end{equation*}
    divide both sides by \(2\)
    \begin{equation*} y = -\frac{4}{2}x + \frac{9}{2} \end{equation*}
    and simplify to
    \begin{equation*} y = -2x + \frac{9}{2}. \end{equation*}
    In this form, we see the slope of the line is \(-2.\)
    By Theorem 2.4.3, the slope of a line parallel to \(4x + 2y = 9\) is \(-2\text{.}\) Now we use Definition 2.3.8 to write down a line with slope \(-2\) through the point \((-1,5)\)
    \begin{equation*} y - 5 = -2(x - (-1)) \end{equation*}
    and simplify
    \begin{equation*} y - 5 = -2(x + 1). \end{equation*}
    Finally, to put this line into The Slope-Intercept Form of a Line, distribute the \(-2\)
    \begin{equation*} y - 5 = -2x - 2, \end{equation*}
    add 5 to both sides of the equation
    \begin{equation*} y = -2x -2 + 5, \end{equation*}
    and simplify
    \begin{equation*} y = -2x + 3. \end{equation*}

    Definition 2.4.6.

    Two lines are perpendicular if they intersect at a right angle.

    Theorem 2.4.8.

    Two lines are perpendicular if and only if the product of their slopes is \(-1\text{.}\)

    Example 2.4.9.

    Determine whether the lines
    \begin{equation*} 3x + 5y = 7 \quad\text{and}\quad -5x + 3y = 21 \end{equation*}
    are perpendicular.
    Solution.
    First we must place the lines in The Slope-Intercept Form of a Line to find the slopes. The first equation becomes
    \begin{align*} 3x + 5y \amp= 7 \amp \amp \text{Subtract}\ 3x\ \text{from both sides}\\ 5y \amp= -3x + 7 \amp\amp \text{Divide both sides by}\ 5\\ y \amp= \frac{-3x + 7}{5} \amp\amp \text{Simplify}\\ y \amp = -\frac{3}{5}x + \frac{7}{5}. \end{align*}
    In this form, we can read off the slope of the line as \(-3/5.\)
    The second equation becomes
    \begin{align*} -5x + 3y \amp = 21 \amp\amp \text{Add}\ 5x\ \text{to both sides}\\ 3y \amp= 5x + 21 \amp\amp \text{Divide both sides by}\ 3\\ y \amp = \frac{5x + 21}{3} \amp\amp \text{Simplify}\\ y \amp = \frac{5}{3}x + 7. \end{align*}
    In this form, we can read off the slop of the line as \(5/3\text{.}\)
    Finally, to check whether these lines are parallel, we compute the product of these slopes. Since
    \begin{equation*} -\frac{3}{5} \times \frac{5}{3} = -\frac{15}{15} = -1 \end{equation*}
    we conclude that these lines are perpendicular by Theorem 2.4.8.

    Example 2.4.10.

    Find the equation of a line perpendicular to \(2x + 3y = 5\) that passes through the point \((3,5)\text{.}\) Write your answer in The Slope-Intercept Form of a Line form.
    Solution.
    First, place the line in The Slope-Intercept Form of a Line
    \begin{align*} 2x + 3y \amp= 5 \amp\amp \text{Subtract}\ 2x\ \text{from both sides}\\ 3y \amp = -2x + 5 \amp\amp \text{Divide both sides by}\ 3\\ y \amp = \frac{-2x + 5}{3} \amp\amp \text{Simplify}\\ y \amp = -\frac{2}{3}x + \frac{5}{3}. \end{align*}
    We can read off the slope as \(-2/3\) from this equation.
    To find the slope, \(m\text{,}\) of a perpendicular line, Theorem 2.4.8 tells us we must solve the equation
    \begin{equation*} -\frac{2}{3} m = -1 \end{equation*}
    for \(m\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} -\frac{2}{3} m \amp= -1 \amp\amp \text{Multiply both sides by}\ -\frac{3}{2}\\ -\frac{3}{2}\left(-\frac{2}{3}m\right) \amp = -\frac{3}{2}\left(-1\right) \amp\amp \text{Simplify}\\ m \amp = \frac{3}{2}. \end{align*}
    Using The Point-Slope Form of a Line, the equation of the line perpendicular to \(2x + 3y = 5\) that passes through the point \((3,5)\) is
    \begin{equation*} y - 5 = \frac{3}{2}(x - 3). \end{equation*}
    Finally, we convert to The Slope-Intercept Form of a Line
    \begin{align*} y - 5 \amp= \frac{3}{2}(x - 3) \amp \amp \text{Distribute}\\ y - 5 \amp = \frac{3}{2}x - \frac{9}{2} \amp\amp \text{Add}\ 5\ \text{to both sides}\\ y \amp = \frac{3}{2}x - \frac{9}{2} + 5 \amp\amp \text{Find a common denominator}\\ y \amp = \frac{3}{2} x - \frac{9}{2} + \frac{10}{2} \amp \amp \text{Simplify}\\ y \amp = \frac{3}{2}x + \frac{1}{2}. \end{align*}
